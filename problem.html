The Problem

People are the real wealth of nations—and education enables them to live healthier, happier and more productive lives. There is broad agreement, backed by research findings, that education enhances people‘s ability to make informed decisions, sustain a livelihood, adopt new technologies, be better parents, cope with shocks, and be responsible citizens and effective stewards of the natural environment. These links between education and the quality of people‘s lives are why the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (1989) recognize everyone‘s right to education.

Accordingly, the government of Uganda introduced a popular education scheme, the Universal Primary Education (UPE) in 1997. The implementation of the UPE scheme resulted in great influx of children into school. Enrolment increased from 2.5 million in 2007 to 8.5 million pupils in 2013 (State of Uganda Population Report 2015).

However, more schooling has not resulted in greater learning for too many boys and girls, especially, from low-income families. Various learning assessments document disappointing learning outcomes as well as illustrate the seriousness of the learning challenge across Universal Primary Schools contexts.

These trends risk perpetuating intergenerational poverty and inequalities and are the very opposite of the nationally and internationally proclaimed high level ambitions. In this case, will Uganda’s “four years away” dream for “Middle Income Status”, Uganda’s Vision 2040 “A Transformed Ugandan Society from a Peasant to a Modern and Prosperous Country within 30 years,” and the UN’s Sustainable Goals “the quality of life of the rural poor improve by 2030” be a reality for the illiterate and semi-literate products of our school system
